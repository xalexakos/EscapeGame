“Self-organized” term

 

At first step, we are going to discuss the term “self-organized”. We regard a system consisting of several entities. We call this system organized if it has a certain structure and functionality.

 

Structure means that the entities are arranged in a particular manner and interact (communicate) with each other in some way. Functionality means that the overall system fulfills a certain purpose. For example, a school of small fish tries to achieve a group structure that protects the fish against enemies.

 

 

A system is self-organized if it is organized without any external or central dedicated control entity. In other words, the individual entities interact directly with each other in a distributed peer-to-peer manner. Interaction between the entities is usually localized. For example, in a school of fish each individual fish bases its behavior on its observation of the position and speed of its immediate neighbors, rather than on the behavior of a “central fish” or that of the whole school.

 

Another important characteristic of self-organized systems is their adaptability with respect to changes in the system or environment. Systems like these, should be reorganized when an internal or external change take place. With this behavior, we can achieve robustness against failure and damage. We can reassure that our system will work as expected or even with a small degradation, avoiding break down. Another important property is that many self-organizing systems show a high level of scalability, which means that the system still works if the number of entities is very large. For instance, the self-organized group formation of fish works well in schools with thousands of fish.

 

Introduction to SON

 

Self-organized networks (SON) is a paradigm defined under the auspices of the 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP) and the Next Generation Mobile Networks (NGMN) Alliance, aiming to automate mobile network operation, administration and management (OAM). Several papers mention three architectures of SONs.

 

ü  Centralized SON: In wireless mobile networks Operation and Management (OAM) network component plays and important role in network’s functionality. It is mainly responsible for performance optimization and fast adaptation in cases of failure recovery or generally changes in network loads, architecture, infrastructure and technology. Even if SON functionality is centralized - so easy to be deployed- , we confront latency to simple optimization cases due to central processing. The complexity of SON systems and the advent of ultra dense networks (UDN), render the aforementioned way of processing inappropriate for 5G networks. The OAM systems are getting more and more decentralised. The long-lasting dilemma has thus been on finding a right balance between centralised control versus distributed SON.

 

ü  Distributed SON: In this kind of SON, optimization algorithms are executed in macrocells e.g eNodeB. In that way, SON functionalities could be found in several locations. This poses the problem of additional overhead to deployment, though. Thus, distributed SON is not suitable for complex optimization schemes, but could be used for quick optimization responses among a small number of BSs.

 

ü  Hybrid SON: This is a combination of above two categories. Complex optimization schemes take place on OAM and quick in macrocell level.

 

The main characteristics of self-organized networks are:

 

ü  Self-configuration: Is relative to self-planning and generally with an automated way of e.g deploying a new site location in dense mobile user areas using automated authentication and verification of each new site.

 

ü  Self-optimization: Is relative to the collection of measurements and performance indicators from user equipment and network elements and auto-tuning network parameters in order to enhance its performance.

 

ü  Self-healing: Probably the most important aspect of SON networks. It has to do with failure detection, diagnosis, compensation and recovery of network. Network should be affected at a minimum degree in order to ensure its robustness.

 

Self-healing is done in two steps: Cell Outage Detection (COD) and Cell Outage Compensation (COC). Both these provide automatic mitigation of BS failures especially when BS is unable to recognize being out of service, thus failed to notify OAM of the outage. There are also cases in which operators have no knowledge of failures until they are notified by customer support. COD ensures that detection and classification of problems will take place, while COC will provide the appropriate actions to alleviate the failure consequences.

 

Latent fault determination term is used when the fault detection is based on evidence, such as abnormality in statistics, rather than an alarm or state change. In cases where OAM indicators suggest that the functionality is ok- but in reality this is not correct-, combination of statistics and activity watchdog timers help to identify the failure. For example statistics of several periods of time are gathered and contribute to the gradual creation of a statistical picture of the expected performance these periods of time e.g weekday, weekends. If the statistics collected deviate from this picture, this might be an indicator of potential failure.
